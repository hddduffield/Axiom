## TIMING OF GIFTS (CRITICAL FOR ANNUAL EXCLUSION)

- Exclusion is **calendar-year**: must be made by December 31 of the gift year
- Unused exclusion does NOT roll forward
- For check delivery: gift is deemed made when the check is **deposited and clears** (or unconditional power passes), not when written. December gift checks must be deposited by year-end to count.
- For brokerage transfers: gift is made when the asset is transferred to the donee's account (not when instructions are submitted)
- For Crummey notice: notice must be given for the trust to receive the present-interest treatment; lapse of withdrawal right is itself the moment of completed gift

---

## PORTABILITY (DSUE)

- Surviving spouse can use deceased spouse's unused exemption (DSUE)
- **Election required:** must be made on Form 706 estate tax return for the deceased spouse, even if no estate tax is owed
- **Filing deadline:** generally 9 months after date of death + 6-month automatic extension; in some cases, late portability available under Rev. Proc. 2022-32 (within 5 years of date of death)
- **DSUE preserved at first death** — surviving spouse can use against own gifts during life or at death
- **Limitation:** DSUE does NOT preserve GST exemption; only basic exclusion
- **Recommended for ALL married couples** at first death, even when first estate is small
- Citation: IRC §2010(c)(4)–(5); Rev. Proc. 2022-32

## §2503(b) ANNUAL EXCLUSION — KEY MECHANICS

- **Present interest required:** gift must give donee an immediate, unrestricted right to use, possession, or enjoyment of the property
- **Crummey power workaround:** beneficiary's right to withdraw trust contributions for a defined period (typically 30–60 days) creates "present interest" and qualifies the gift for the annual exclusion
- **Direct payments for tuition or medical care** — exempt under IRC §2503(e); do NOT count against annual exclusion
  - Tuition: must be paid directly to qualified educational institution; covers tuition only (not room/board/books)
  - Medical: must be paid directly to provider; covers medical care including LTC premiums
  - These exemptions are unlimited in amount

---

## §2503(c) MINOR'S TRUST

- Alternative to Crummey for minors
- Gift to trust qualifies for annual exclusion if:
  - Property and income may be expended for the beneficiary before age 21
  - Property and any accumulated income passes to beneficiary at age 21
  - In the event of beneficiary's death before 21, property is payable to beneficiary's estate or under a general POA
- Less flexible than Crummey trust for HNW planning (the firm typically prefers Crummey-noticed trusts with extended ages)

## CRUMMEY POWER MECHANICS

See `09_crummey_mechanics.md` for full detail. Summary:

- Trustee provides written notice to each beneficiary on each contribution
- Notice must explain: amount of gift, beneficiary's withdrawal right, deadline (typically 30–60 days)
- Documentation: written notice + return receipt or acknowledgment + file copy
- After demand period lapses, contribution is deemed completed gift to trust
- **Lapse limit (5/$5,000 rule):** lapse over the greater of $5,000 or 5% of trust corpus is itself a taxable gift by the beneficiary back to the trust (general power of appointment)
- **Hanging Crummey power:** preserves withdrawal right indefinitely until lapse limit clears, avoiding the lapse-as-gift problem
- Citation: Crummey v. Commissioner, 397 F.2d 82 (9th Cir. 1968); IRC §2514

## §2036 / §2038 — RETAINED INTEREST INCLUSION (CRITICAL FOR PLANNING)

These are the rules that include otherwise-transferred property back in the grantor's taxable estate when the grantor retained certain interests or powers. Defective IDGT planning depends on triggering grantor-trust income tax rules WITHOUT triggering §2036/§2038 estate inclusion.

- **§2036:** transfers with retained life estate, retained right to designate beneficiaries, retained income — included in estate
- **§2038:** transfers where grantor retained power to alter, amend, revoke, or terminate — included in estate
- **§2042:** life insurance with incidents of ownership — included in estate
- **§2035:** 3-year lookback for transfers of life insurance and certain other transfers; inclusion if death within 3 years
- **Safe harbor for IDGT:** grantor-trust status under §675(4) (power to substitute assets of equivalent value, exercisable in non-fiduciary capacity) does NOT trigger §2036/§2038 — this is the workhorse mechanism

Citation: IRC §§2035, 2036, 2038, 2042; Rev. Rul. 85-13

## §2702 SPECIAL VALUATION RULES (RELEVANT TO GRAT)

- General rule: when an interest in trust is transferred to a family member and the transferor retains an interest, the retained interest is valued at zero (forcing the gift to be the full FMV of the transferred property)
- **GRAT exception:** if the retained interest is a "qualified annuity interest" — fixed annuity, paid at least annually, for a fixed term — the retained interest is valued using §7520 rate
- **Walton GRAT (zeroed-out):** Walton v. Commissioner, 115 T.C. 589 (2000) confirmed that an annuity continuing for the lesser of the term or the grantor's life is valued at full annuity, allowing zero-gift GRAT when annuity properly sized
- **Citation:** IRC §2702; Treas. Reg. §25.2702-3; Walton v. Commissioner, 115 T.C. 589 (2000); Rev. Rul. 2008-22

---

## §2704 SPECIAL VALUATION RULES (RELEVANT TO FLP / RECAP)

- **§2704(a):** lapsing voting/liquidation rights treated as transfers
- **§2704(b):** restrictions on liquidation that are "applicable restrictions" (more restrictive than state law default) are disregarded for valuation
- **Practical impact:** family-only entities cannot create artificial liquidation restrictions to inflate discounts; market-based restrictions in arm's-length agreements are respected
- **Proposed 2016 regulations:** would have severely limited valuation discounts; **withdrawn in 2017** under prior administration; **NOT reinstated under OBBBA**; current law allows traditional discount approaches subject to qualified appraisal
- Citation: IRC §2704; proposed regulations REG-163113-02 (withdrawn)

## VALUATION DISCOUNT TYPICAL RANGES (FOR NON-VOTING / MINORITY INTERESTS)

| Discount Type | Typical Range | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Lack of Marketability (LOM / DLOM) | 15%–35% | Driven by absence of market for transferable interest |
| Lack of Control / Minority Interest | 10%–25% | Driven by inability to direct entity activity |
| **Combined (when both apply)** | **25%–40%** | Typical defensible range for non-voting LLC/holdco interest with qualified appraisal |
| Aggressive (audit-bait) | >40% | Requires exceptional facts to defend |

**Defensibility = qualified appraisal.** Discounts cited without an appraisal supporting them have no value in audit. The firm's house position: never cite a specific discount %  in the plan itself; cite the engagement of a qualified appraiser who will determine the supportable discount.

## ESTATE-TAX LIQUIDITY

- **§6166 deferral:** estate tax attributable to closely-held business interest can be deferred up to 14 years (5 years interest-only, then 10 years installments) at a reduced interest rate; available when closely-held business is >35% of adjusted gross estate
- **Stock redemption under §303:** corporation can redeem stock from estate to pay estate tax + funeral/admin without dividend treatment, when redemption ≤ federal estate tax + funeral/admin expenses
- **§2032A special-use valuation:** real estate used in family farm or business can be valued at its actual use rather than fair-market value, reducing estate (subject to limits)
- These are post-death tools; planning should not rely on them but should know they exist

## OBBBA-RELATED FOOTNOTES

- The OBBBA preservation of the higher exemption is "permanent" but Congress can change tax law at any time. The planning case for using exemption now still has validity:
  - Future Congress could lower exemption
  - Inflation adjustment is modest; large gifts now lock in current exemption usage
  - For older or health-compromised clients, freezing appreciation outside the estate via gifts has irreversible benefit even if exemption later declines
- **Use-it-or-lose-it concern is dampened (not eliminated)** by the OBBBA permanence
