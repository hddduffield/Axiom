## TRIGGERING CONDITIONS

### Structured logic
```
TRIGGER if ALL of:
  - Liquidity event > $25M making foundation administratively justified
  - Charitable intent at scale ($1M+/year ongoing)
  - Family willing to engage with foundation governance

DISQUALIFY if:
  - DAF (REC-CHR-001) sufficient for needs
  - Family unwilling to engage
  - Insufficient scale (< $5M endowment) — DAF more efficient
```

### Natural-language explanation
A private foundation is a 501(c)(3) entity privately funded and family-controlled. Greater control than DAF (employees, programs, direct involvement) but more administrative burden, lower deduction limits (30% AGI cash; 20% appreciated property), 5% annual distribution requirement, and excise taxes on net investment income (1.39% post-2020). Suited to substantial-scale ongoing philanthropy.

### Hard disqualifiers
- Below scale threshold
- Family disengagement

## WHAT IT IS
Private foundation: 501(c)(3) operating as grantmaker (most common — "private non-operating foundation") or as direct service provider (less common — "private operating foundation"). Family controls board; family employs staff (or contracts); family makes grant decisions.

Compared to DAF: more control; more administration; lower AGI deduction limits; 5% minimum payout; excise tax on net investment income.

## WHY WE RECOMMEND IT (when triggered)
At scale, families want direct engagement with grantmaking, employee philanthropy, multi-generational involvement. Foundation provides that vehicle. DAF works for smaller / less-engaged charitable intent.

## VARIATIONS
- **Non-operating (grantmaking):** standard structure
- **Operating:** runs programs directly; fewer payout restrictions; rarer

## QUANTIFIED IMPACT FRAMEWORK

### Components
- Annual grantmaking (5% minimum payout)
- Foundation investment growth
- Family engagement and education
- Excise tax (1.39% on net investment income)

## IMPLEMENTATION STEPS
1. Decision: foundation vs. DAF (DAF first for many; foundation if scale + engagement)
2. Specialist counsel — foundation formation
3. State filing (typically nonprofit corporation) and IRS Form 1023
4. Initial funding (cash; some appreciated assets work, foundation-specific limits)
5. Board structure (family + perhaps independent directors)
6. Investment policy
7. Annual Form 990-PF, audit, board meetings

## COMMON MISTAKES
- Self-dealing violations (family member as employee with above-market comp; benefit transactions)
- 5% minimum payout failure (penalty + excise tax)
- Excess business holdings (family business interests held by foundation)
- Inadequate documentation of grants

## COORDINATION NOTES
- **PSA Wealth:** investment management
- **CPA:** Form 990-PF; specialist
- **Attorney:** specialist nonprofit / private foundation counsel
- **Foundation administrator:** if engaged

## CLIENT CONVERSATION FRAMING
> "At your scale of ongoing charitable intent — $${annual_giving}+ per year — a private foundation provides direct engagement that a DAF can't. You hire family or staff, run programs, control investment, build a multi-generational philanthropy entity. Trade-offs: more admin (Form 990-PF, audit, board), lower AGI deduction limits, 5% minimum payout, 1.39% excise tax on investment income. We typically recommend starting with DAF and transitioning to foundation as scale and family engagement grow."

## CAVEATS & DISQUALIFIERS
- Self-dealing rules strict
- 5% minimum payout
- Excise tax
- Specialist counsel mandatory
- Multi-year commitment
