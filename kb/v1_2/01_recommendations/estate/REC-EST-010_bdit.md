# [REC-EST-010] — Beneficiary-Defective Inheritor's Trust (BDIT)

## METADATA
- **ID:** REC-EST-010
- **Status:** Advanced
- **Category:** Estate
- **Engagement archetypes:** Pre-Liquidity-Founder, Pre-Exit (early-stage clients)
- **Last verified:** April 2026

---

## TRIGGERING CONDITIONS

### Structured logic
```
TRIGGER if ALL of:
  - Client is the BENEFICIARY of trust funded by parent or other older generation
  - Client wants to transfer asset out of own estate
  - Asset is not yet in client's hands (e.g., parent or grandparent willing to fund seed)
  - Sophisticated multi-generational structure desired
```

### Natural-language explanation
A BDIT is a trust funded by an older generation (e.g., parent) for a beneficiary (the client/founder). The beneficiary holds a §678 power that makes the trust "defective" as to the beneficiary for income-tax purposes. Beneficiary can sell appreciating assets to the BDIT in exchange for a note (similar to IDGT mechanics) without recognizing gain.

Result: assets the beneficiary owns (or would acquire) flow through the BDIT and out of the beneficiary's estate.

### Hard disqualifiers
- No older-generation funder available
- §678 mechanics not properly drafted
- Specialist counsel unavailable

---

## WHAT IT IS

A BDIT is structurally similar to an IDGT but with a critical difference: the trust is "defective" as to the beneficiary (the client/founder), not as to the grantor (parent or older generation who funded the trust). The beneficiary holds a §678 withdrawal power over the seed gift, which causes the trust to be a grantor trust as to the beneficiary for income-tax purposes only.

Because the trust is a grantor trust as to the beneficiary, sales between beneficiary and trust are not recognized for income tax (similar to IDGT mechanics). But because the beneficiary did not fund the trust (the parent did), the trust's assets are not in the beneficiary's estate.

---

## WHY WE RECOMMEND IT

For pre-liquidity founders or clients early in business growth: a BDIT can be the receiving vehicle for early-stage equity (low value) that grows substantially. By selling at low value to BDIT, future appreciation occurs in trust outside beneficiary's estate. Particularly powerful when:
- Founder has not yet built a large estate
- An older-generation funder is available
- Asset has very high appreciation expectation

Limited applicability in PSA's typical lane (pre-exit owners with established business and substantial own estate). Most useful for very-early-stage clients or for grandchildren-as-beneficiaries planning.

---

## QUANTIFIED IMPACT FRAMEWORK

Highly fact-specific. For a successful BDIT funded with seed at company's early stage:
- Founder sells $5M of stock to BDIT for note at AFR
- Stock grows to $50M+ over 7 years
- $45M+ of growth outside founder's estate
- Federal estate tax avoided at 40%: $18M+

---

## IMPLEMENTATION STEPS

1. **Older-generation funder** (parent, grandparent) seeds trust with cash gift (typically $5K-$50K)
2. **Beneficiary holds §678 withdrawal right** for limited window (typically 30-60 days)
3. **Trust documents drafted with specific §678 power** triggering grantor-trust status as to beneficiary
4. **Beneficiary sells appreciating asset to BDIT** in exchange for AFR-bearing note (similar to IDGT mechanics)
5. **Annual administration** — note interest, asset performance tracking
6. **Coordinate with broader estate plan** — BDIT can have generation-skipping features

## COMMON MISTAKES & AUDIT TRIGGERS
- **§678 mechanics improperly drafted** — IRS attacks grantor-trust status
- **Beneficiary funded the trust** — collapses entire structure
- **Inadequate seed amount** — IRS argues no economic substance
- **Beneficiary as trustee** — exclusion compromised
