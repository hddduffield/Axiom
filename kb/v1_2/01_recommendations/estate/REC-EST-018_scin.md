---

## TRIGGERING CONDITIONS

### Structured logic
```
TRIGGER if ALL of:
  - Donor over 60+ with shortened life expectancy considerations
  - Asset to transfer that will appreciate
  - Donor willing to bear risk of accelerated death (no further benefit if dies before note pays)
```

### Natural-language explanation
Sale to family member or trust in exchange for installment note that "self-cancels" at donor's death. Premium paid for cancellation feature (above standard AFR). If donor dies before note pays out, balance is forgiven and assets are outside estate.

### Hard disqualifiers
- Donor in robust health (premium not justified)
- Asset doesn't appreciate

---

## WHAT IT IS

A SCIN is an installment note with a clause: at donor's death (during note term), remaining balance is automatically cancelled — assets stay with buyer (typically family trust) without estate inclusion. Donor charges a "risk premium" above standard AFR to compensate for the cancellation possibility.

If donor lives to maturity: standard installment sale outcome.
If donor dies during term: remaining balance forgiven; assets fully transferred without estate tax.

---

## WHY WE RECOMMEND IT

For older or health-compromised donors who still have substantial assets to transfer, SCIN offers a "win-win" structure: standard installment if healthy; full transfer if not.

PSA's house position: niche tool. Use only when health considerations make the cancellation feature genuinely valuable.

---

## QUANTIFIED IMPACT FRAMEWORK

### Worked example
- Donor age 75, asset value $5M
- 9-year SCIN at AFR + premium (premium calculated actuarially)
- If donor dies in year 4: remaining balance ~$3M cancelled; $3M of asset transfers free of estate tax
- Estate tax saved at 40%: $1.2M

---

## IMPLEMENTATION STEPS

1. Actuarial analysis of mortality risk (basis for premium calculation)
2. Note drafted with self-cancellation clause
3. Premium above AFR established (must be "fair" to avoid gift element)
4. Sale documented; note executed
5. Annual interest and principal payments
6. At donor's death, note cancellation documented

## COMMON MISTAKES & AUDIT TRIGGERS
- **Inadequate premium** — IRS attacks as gift
- **Donor in poor health at funding** (Davidson v. Commissioner, T.C. 2009-26 — sham analysis)
- **§7520 mortality tables for terminally ill** — may not be allowed; case-specific actuarial required
